II.5 Building Operations Financing


Financing – Building Operations (II.5.)

Building acquisition requires debt and equity capital.

  • Typical Building operators purchase the completed buildings with a combination of debt and equity capital.
  • Large financial institutions or public REITs pay acquire the properties will all cash and finance the properties using a financial strategy at the corporate level (like bonds).

A long-term loans will provide for holdbacks for unleased space.

  • Tenant improvements
  • Leasing commissions

Short-term loan(s) may provide for cash advances and interest accrual during lease-up.

  • These are temporary financing arrangement that require personal guarantees.
  • Lender may call these working capital loans.
  • Equity partners may make “partnership loans.”

Second mortgage, gap financing, and mezzanine loan can help cover the lease-up or re-leasing risk.

  • These are more formal loan arrangements.
  • Second mortgage and gap financing require liens on the property.
  • Mezzanine loans are secured by the partnership equity ownership so that formal default procedures are not required in the event of default.

Equity partners can have “capital calls” to cover lease-up and re-leasing cash flow deficits.

  • Equity partner may be subject to capital calls in the event of negative cash flows.
  • These capital calls should be a last resort.
  • Equity reserves for negative cash flows should funded at the initial property acquisition or out of positive cash flows experienced before the negative cash flows.

Different types of equity partners may have different distribution priorities.

  • Not all equity partners are treated equally.
  • There may be different classes of equity partners with different risk exposures.
  • Lower risk levels can be obtained by giving preferential returns to some equity investors.
  • Investors with higher risks usually get higher shares of the cash flows after the preferences are met.
